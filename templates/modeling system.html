System Modeling

System modeling is the foundation for understanding, simulating, and controlling pneumatic systems. In simple terms, it transforms the physical behavior of actuators into mathematical representations we can analyze, simulate, and improve.

Physical Setup

To understand how pneumatic systems behave in real-world applications, we start by modeling their physical characteristics using fundamental principles of dynamics and control.

This physical setup is analogous to a mass-spring-damper system, a classic representation of mechanical systems. Here's what each component represents:

Mass (M): Represents the load or inertia of the system.

Spring (C): Represents the restoring force that acts to return the system to its equilibrium position.

Damper (D): Represents the resistance to motion, which dissipates energy and slows down the system.

Displacement (x(t)): Shows how far the mass moves over time as a result of the applied force.

Force (F): In a pneumatic system, this is usually the force generated by pressurized air acting on a piston.

The motion of the mass is governed by Newton’s Second Law, resulting in a second-order differential equation:

\[ M\frac{d^2x(t)}{dt^2} + D\frac{dx(t)}{dt} + Cx(t) = F(t) \]

System Parameters

For this example, let's assume that the parameters of the system are:

Fixed value:

Cross-sectional area of the piston (A) = 0.0314 mm2

Variable value:

Mass (M) = 20 kg

Damping Coefficient (D) = 60 Ns/m

Spring Constant (C) = 100 N/m

Pressure (P) = 20684.28 Pa

System Equations

To simulate and control the behavior of a pneumatic actuator, we derive its mathematical model based on Newton’s Second Law and Laplace Transform techniques.

Using the Laplace Transform, we can convert the differential equation into an algebraic equation in the s-domain:

\[ M\frac{d^2X(s)}{ds^2} + D\frac{dX(s)}{ds} + CX(s) = F(s) \]

\[ (Ms^2 + Ds + C)X(s) = F(s) \]

Since the force comes from the pressure (P(s)) acting on area (A), we substitute:

\[ F(s) = P(s)A \]

\[ (Ms^2 + Ds + C)X(s) = P(s)A \]

Thus, the final transfer function becomes:

\[ T(s) = \frac{X(s)}{P(s)} = \frac{A}{Ms^2 + Ds + C}\]
